Hong Kong Travel Deals: How to Save on Taxi Rides

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

Hong Kong is a city of contrasts—soaring skyscrapers against lush mountains, traditional dai pai dongs next to Michelin-starred restaurants, and serene temples amidst bustling shopping districts. While the city boasts one of the world’s most efficient public transport systems, sometimes only a taxi will do. Whether you’re rushing to a dim sum reservation, heading back to your hotel with shopping bags, or trying to reach that hidden Instagrammable spot, taxis are a convenient part of the Hong Kong experience. But they can also add up quickly, eating into your travel budget. Fear not—this guide will show you how to navigate Hong Kong’s taxi system like a pro and save money while doing it.

Understanding Hong Kong's Taxi System

Before we dive into money-saving tips, it’s essential to understand how taxis work in Hong Kong. Unlike many cities where taxis are uniformly priced, Hong Kong taxis are color-coded and serve different zones.

The Three Types of Hong Kong Taxis

Hong Kong taxis are divided into three colors: red, green, and blue. - Urban (Red) Taxis: These are the most common and can operate almost anywhere in Hong Kong, including Hong Kong Island, Kowloon, and the New Territories. They are your go-to for most inner-city rides. - New Territories (Green) Taxis: These are restricted primarily to the New Territories and are not permitted to pick up passengers in urban areas. Fares are slightly lower than red taxis. - Lantau (Blue) Taxis: These operate exclusively on Lantau Island and serve areas like the airport, Ngong Ping 360, and Tai O Fishing Village. Their fares are the highest of the three.

Knowing which taxi to hail can prevent awkward situations with drivers and ensure you’re not overpaying for a ride that could have been done with a cheaper green taxi if you’re in the right zone.

How Fares Are Calculated

The taxi meter is your friend. The initial flagfall for a red taxi is HKD $27 for the first two kilometers, after which it’s approximately HKD $1.9 for every 200 meters. There are additional charges for tolls, tunnels, and luggage (usually HKD $6 per piece stored in the trunk). Always ensure the driver resets the meter at the start of your journey.

Smart Strategies to Save on Every Ride

Now for the good part—how to keep more cash in your pocket for more egg tarts and pineapple buns.

1. Use Ride-Hailing Apps: The 21st-Century Solution

While Uber operates in Hong Kong, it’s not the only player. Local apps like HKTaxi are incredibly popular and connect you with licensed taxi drivers. Here’s how they save you money: - Upfront Pricing: Some apps offer fixed fares for popular routes, so you know the cost before you even get in the car. No surprises. - Promo Codes and Welcome Offers: New users often get discounts on their first few rides. Search for "Hong Kong taxi promo code" before your trip. - No Hailing Stress: During peak hours or rainy days, hailing a cab on the street can be impossible. Apps guarantee you a ride, saving you time and frustration.

2. Master the Art of the Short Ride

Taxis are most cost-effective for short to medium distances. For longer journeys, consider alternatives. For example: - Taking the Airport Express train to the city center (Hong Kong or Kowloon Station) and then a short taxi ride to your hotel is almost always cheaper than a direct taxi from the airport. - Use taxis for "last-mile" solutions. The MTR (subway) and buses will get you 90% of the way there cheaply and efficiently; a taxi can cover that final, tricky distance.

3. Avoid Peak Hours and Tunnel Traffic

Hong Kong’s traffic is legendary, and the meter keeps ticking while you’re stuck in it. If possible, avoid taxi rides during: - Morning and Evening Rush Hour (7:30-9:30 AM & 5:00-7:30 PM) - When Tunnels are Congested: Cross-harbor tunnel tolls are added to your fare, and the waiting time can be expensive. Sometimes, taking the MTR across the harbor and then a taxi is faster and cheaper.

4. Travel in Small Groups

A taxi ride becomes incredibly economical when split between 3 or 4 people. The cost per person for a HKD $100 ride is only HKD $25-33, which is often cheaper and far more direct than multiple MTR tickets.

5. Know the Landmarks

Many taxi drivers speak limited English. Having your destination written in Chinese or saved as a pin on Google Maps is helpful. Even better, know a major landmark near your destination. Instead of saying "18 Tai Ping Shan Street," say "near Man Mo Temple." This prevents wrong turns and unnecessary extra meters on the fare.

Beyond Taxis: Integrating with Hong Kong's Travel Deals

Your transportation strategy shouldn’t exist in a vacuum. The real savings come from combining taxi rides with Hong Kong’s fantastic tourist offers.

Leverage the MTR Tourist Day Pass

For HK$65, you get unlimited rides on the MTR for one day (excluding the Airport Express and certain first-class upgrades). Use this to cover the bulk of your travel. Then, use taxis strategically for trips that are inconvenient by rail, like going to the Peak Tram terminus or Stanley Market.

The Octopus Card is Your Best Friend

This reloadable smart card is used for almost all public transport. While you can’t use it to pay for every taxi, you can use it for MTR, buses, ferries, and even in convenience stores. - Pro Tip: The On-Loan Octopus card costs HK$150 (HK$100 credit + HK$50 refundable deposit). Use it to avoid fumbling for change on buses and trains, making your multi-modal travel seamless. Save your cash for taxis that don’t accept cards.

Look for Hotel and Attraction Packages

Many hotel packages, especially at resorts like Disneyland Hong Kong or on the outlying islands, include shuttle bus services. Always check what free transport is offered before booking a taxi. A shuttle bus is the ultimate travel deal—it costs nothing extra.

Cultural Etiquette and Practical Tips for a Smooth Ride

Saving money isn’t just about fares; it’s about having a smooth, efficient experience.

  • Cash is King: While some taxis now accept card payments, the vast majority are cash-only. Always carry a sufficient amount of Hong Kong dollars in smaller denominations.
  • Tipping: It is not mandatory or expected in Hong Kong taxis. Simply round up to the nearest dollar as a gesture of appreciation. For a HK$78 fare, paying HK$80 is perfectly fine.
  • Red Taxis are Everywhere: For most tourists, sticking to red taxis is the simplest and most flexible option. Don’t go out of your way to find a green or blue taxi unless you are specifically in their service region.
  • Seatbelts are Mandatory: It’s the law for all passengers to wear seatbelts.
